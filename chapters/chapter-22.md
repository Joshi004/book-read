# Chapter 22 — Charisma

> *"Charisma is a certain quality of an individual personality by virtue of which he is set apart from ordinary men and treated as endowed with supernatural, superhuman, or at least specifically exceptional powers or qualities."* — Max Weber (1978)<!-- Citation: Max Weber, "Economy and Society" (Wirtschaft und Gesellschaft), Roth & Wittich eds., University of California Press, 1978 — the standard English-language translation and the citation form used throughout the academic literature on Weberian charisma. Back in Chapter 12, authority, charisma, and composure were named as a master key to the human mind. Composure closed out the last chapter. Now it's charisma's turn — approached not as a fixed personality trait, but through the lens of crowd psychology: what a charismatic person actually does to the people standing in front of them.

---

## The Weberian Definition

In its simplest form, charisma is defined by Weber as a certain quality of an individual personality by virtue of which a person is set apart from ordinary people and treated as endowed with supernatural, superhuman, or at least specifically exceptional powers or qualities (Weber, 1978).

::: definition
**Charisma** — a quality of individual personality that sets a person apart from the ordinary, and causes them to be treated as endowed with supernatural, superhuman, or specifically exceptional powers.
:::

Weber portrays individuals possessing charisma as, above all else, emotional and vitalizing. This stands in complete opposition to the traditional authority of the patriarch and the rational efficiency of the technician-bureaucrat.<!-- ASR? verify: transcribed as "the innovating authority of the patriarch" — corrected to "the traditional authority of the patriarch." ## Whatever the Leader Says Is Right

Because charismatic authority needs no justification beyond the leader's own person, the orders it produces don't have to hold together.

::: callout
**Orders can be completely whimsical, self-contradictory, and even lead to death or destruction for the follower** — and still be obeyed. This demonstrates the disciples' inner emotional compulsion to obey, without regard for coherence or consequence.
:::

Charismatic figures reveal their capacities through a highly intensified and emotionally labile state of consciousness — one that excites and awes onlookers, and jolts them from the everyday.<!-- Citation: This characterization closely tracks Charles Lindholm's synthesis of Weber in "Charisma, Crowd Psychology and Altered States of Consciousness," Culture, Medicine and Psychiatry, vol. 16 (1992), pp. 287–310 — verified via web search, which returned this near-identical passage. ## How Charisma Is Perceived

Whether someone is seen as charismatic or not can be largely determined by how they communicate. Someone who communicates with others in a way that makes them feel understood, and uses their language, will be more likely to be seen as charismatic (Sorrentino & Murphy, 2003).<!-- ASR? verify: citation transcribed as "Sorrentino and Murphy, 2003." ## Key Takeaways

- **Weber defines charisma as a quality that sets a person apart from the ordinary** — treated as endowed with supernatural, superhuman, or specifically exceptional powers (Weber, 1978).
- **Charismatic authority stands opposed to two other bases of authority**: the traditional authority of the patriarch (grounded in precedent) and the rational efficiency of the bureaucrat (grounded in logic). A charismatic leader's orders are right simply because the leader says so.
- **Because charismatic authority needs no external justification, its orders can be whimsical, self-contradictory, or even destructive** — and still be obeyed, revealing the disciples' inner emotional compulsion to follow regardless of coherence or consequence.
- **Charismatic figures reveal their capacities through a highly intensified, emotionally labile state of consciousness** that excites and awes onlookers, jolting them out of the everyday.
- **Whether you're seen as charismatic is largely shaped by how you communicate.** Making others feel understood, and speaking in their language, increases the likelihood of being perceived as charismatic (Sorrentino & Murphy, 2003).
